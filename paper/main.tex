\begin{abstract}
Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) pipelines typically embed raw document text into a vector database. An alternative---preprocessing documents into structured Markdown before indexing---promises better chunk coherence, preserved table structure, and clearer section boundaries. We benchmark both strategies across four models (Gemma~3~4B, Nemotron~3, DeepSeek~V4~Flash, Gemma~4~26B) on a 75-question corpus spanning 20 arXiv papers (10 well-known, 5 obscure, 5 synthetic with invented facts). All four models consistently prefer raw text over Markdown ($-$3.6\% to $-$6.0\% on the base corpus). We identify the mechanism: 25.3\% of retrieved Markdown chunks are \emph{shallow}---section headers and table markup with $\leq$200 informative characters---that compete with substantive chunks via cosine similarity, producing embedding false positives. Filtering these chunks partially recovers the loss (+0.07 to +0.17). We confirm robustness across two embedding models ($\Delta$ = 0.12/5.0) and two scoring methods (fuzzy overlap and LLM-as-judge). On an expanded 20-document corpus, the Raw advantage narrows ($\Delta$ +0.30 $\rightarrow$ +0.13, n=75, p=0.389) but persists directionally. Bootstrap (10k resamples) and permutation tests provide non-parametric confidence intervals and p-values. An oracle test reveals a +67--85\% retrieval ceiling. These findings link document preprocessing to a specific retrieval failure mechanism and indicate that raw text extraction is simpler, faster, and at least as effective as Markdown preprocessing for typical RAG systems.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)~\citep{lewis2020retrieval} has become a standard architecture for grounding large language model (LLM) outputs in external knowledge. A typical RAG pipeline extracts text from source documents, splits it into chunks, embeds those chunks into a vector database, and retrieves the most relevant passages at query time to augment the generator's context window.

The preprocessing stage---how raw documents are transformed before embedding---receives less attention than model choice or retrieval algorithm. Most pipelines operate on raw extracted text. An alternative, which we call \emph{knowledge compilation}, converts source PDFs into structured Markdown before indexing: section headers become heading markers, tables become pipe-separated grids, and metadata (page numbers, artifacts) are removed. The intuition is straightforward: if the embedding space better reflects document structure, retrieval quality should improve.

We test this intuition across three progressively stronger experimental conditions. On a base corpus of 10 well-known arXiv papers with 50 questions, three pipelines---Raw (PDF text extracted directly), Markdown (PDF $\rightarrow$ structured Markdown compiled by a rule-based parser), and Filtered-Markdown (Markdown with shallow chunks removed)---are evaluated with four language models (Gemma~3~4B, Nemotron~3~Super~Free, DeepSeek~V4~Flash, Gemma~4~26B). We then verify robustness along three dimensions: (1) a second embedding model (BAAI/bge-small-en-v1.5) confirms the pipeline rankings are stable; (2) an LLM-as-judge scoring method provides an independent quality metric; and (3) the corpus is expanded to 20 documents (adding 5 obscure arXiv papers and 5 synthetic documents with invented facts) and 75 questions to test whether the effect generalizes beyond the original document set.

Our findings are consistent across all conditions:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item All four models prefer raw text over Markdown ($-$3.6\% to $-$6.0\%) on the base corpus.
    \item The mechanism is \textbf{shallow chunks}: 25.3\% of retrieved Markdown chunks are informationally empty structural fragments that the embedding model matches via lexical keyword overlap.
    \item Removing these shallow chunks (Pipeline C) partially recovers the loss (+0.07 to +0.17 across three models), confirming a causal role.
    \item The result is robust to embedding model choice ($\Delta$ = 0.12/5.0 between all-MiniLM-L6-v2 and BAAI/bge-small-en-v1.5) and scoring method (fuzzy overlap vs.\ LLM judge, Pearson $r$ = 0.25--0.48).
    \item On the expanded 20-document corpus the Raw advantage narrows but persists directionally ($\Delta$ = +0.13, p=0.389), and Pipeline C shows no benefit, underscoring that shallow-chunk prevalence depends on document composition.
\end{enumerate}

The paper makes four contributions:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item A \textbf{4-model, 3-pipeline benchmark} consistently showing Markdown hurts retrieval quality, with shallow chunks as the identified mechanism.
    \item The \textbf{formalization of shallow chunks} as a retrieval failure mode unique to structured preprocessing, with causal evidence from the filtered pipeline (Pipeline C).
    \item A \textbf{multi-factorial robustness check} spanning embedding model, scoring method, and corpus expansion, establishing the boundaries of the finding.
    \item A \textbf{public reproducible benchmark} with 75 questions, 20 documents, 4 generators, and 2 scoring methods, all released at \url{https://github.com/cioffiAI/rag-vs-markdown}.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}

\subsection{Document Preprocessing for RAG}

The impact of chunking strategy on retrieval quality is well-studied. \citet{gao2023retrieval} compare chunk sizes and overlap for academic question answering. \citet{chen2024dense} evaluate semantic vs.\ fixed-size chunking. However, most work treats chunking as a downstream parameter of the embedding model, not as a \emph{document transformation} that alters information structure before embedding. Our focus is the document representation format itself---raw vs.\ Markdown---which differs from chunk-size tuning in that it changes \emph{what text sequences exist to be chunked}.

Markdown preprocessing for RAG appears in practitioner guides~\citep{openai2024rag,cursor2024} and data-loading libraries such as LlamaIndex~\citep{llamaindex}, which offer PDF-to-Markdown converters. To our knowledge, no controlled experimental comparison quantifies how this transformation interacts with generator capability across multiple robustness dimensions.

\subsection{Embedding False Positives in Dense Retrieval}

Dense retrieval models encode text into a continuous vector space where cosine similarity approximates semantic relevance~\citep{karpukhin2020dense}. However, the embedding space can be misled by lexical overlap: \citet{reimers2019sentence} note that sentence embeddings capture surface-form similarity even when semantic content differs. Our shallow chunk finding instantiates this: Markdown headers are lexically rich but semantically empty, and the embedding model retrieves them preferentially.

\subsection{Model Scale and Instruction Following}

Larger models generally perform better on RAG benchmarks~\citep{lewis2020retrieval,izacard2022atlas}. However, the interaction between model scale and document format is under-explored. \citet{liu2024lost} find that larger models ``get lost'' in long contexts, suggesting that format-induced token overhead (such as Markdown markup) may disproportionately affect models that are otherwise capable.

\subsection{Error Analysis in RAG}

Error taxonomies for RAG exist at both the retrieval and generation levels~\citep{barnett2024seven,es2023ragas}. Our work adopts a 7-code taxonomy (E01--E07) that separates retrieval errors from generation errors, consistent with frameworks proposed by \citet{saad-falcon2023aragog} and \citet{yang2024crag}. We extend these by adding an oracle-condition test that bounds the retrieval contribution.

\section{Experimental Setup}

\subsection{Pipelines}

We compare three document preprocessing pipelines (Figure~\ref{fig:pipelines}):

\begin{description}
    \item[Pipeline A (Raw):] PDF $\rightarrow$ PyMuPDF extraction $\rightarrow$ raw text $\rightarrow$ paragraph-preserving chunking $\rightarrow$ embedding $\rightarrow$ ChromaDB (cosine). Produces 633 chunks (20 documents).
    \item[Pipeline B (Markdown):] PDF $\rightarrow$ PyMuPDF extraction $\rightarrow$ rule-based Markdown compilation (headings, tables, lists) $\rightarrow$ paragraph-preserving chunking $\rightarrow$ embedding $\rightarrow$ ChromaDB (cosine). Produces 664 chunks.
    \item[Pipeline C (MD-filtered):] Same as Pipeline B, but chunks with $\leq$200 informative characters (after stripping Markdown syntax) are removed before embedding. Keeps 647 of 664 chunks (17 filtered, 2.6\%).
\end{description}

All pipelines use the same chunker: a paragraph-preserving algorithm that accumulates whole paragraphs into a buffer and closes it when the next paragraph would exceed 1,500 characters. Single paragraphs longer than 1,500 characters are stored as standalone chunks. The nominal chunk size is therefore a closing threshold, not a hard limit. Overlap is 150 characters.

The Markdown compiler (\texttt{compile\_markdown.py}) identifies section headers from font-size and position heuristics, reconstructs tables from spatial alignment, and strips PDF artifacts. It is a deterministic, rule-based transformation with no learned components.

The shallow chunk filter for Pipeline C uses a threshold of 200 informative characters, where \emph{informative length} is computed by stripping all Markdown syntax tokens (\texttt{\#}, \texttt{*}, \texttt{`}, \texttt{|}, \texttt{---}, \texttt{[}, \texttt{]}, \texttt{>}) and whitespace, then counting the remaining characters. A sensitivity sweep across thresholds T=100--300 confirmed that T=200 is the effective separation point (no additional chunks exist between 200--300 chars).

\subsection{Corpus and Questions}

The base corpus consists of 10 well-known arXiv papers spanning NLP, computer vision, and evaluation (Appendix~\ref{sec:corpus}). The expanded corpus (used in robustness checks) adds 5 obscure arXiv papers ($<$50 citations: REPLUG, HyDE, RAPTOR, In-Context RALM, LLM-Augmenter) and 5 synthetic documents with invented facts (Zorvian Retrieval Protocol, Neuro-Symbolic Index Fusion, Quantum Embedding Projection, Temporal RAG, Adversarial Chunk Filter). The synthetic documents are structured like real papers but contain entirely artificial claims, allowing us to test whether the LLM retrieves from context rather than parametric knowledge.

We constructed 75 gold-standard questions in Italian (to isolate RAG performance from the LLM's English parametric knowledge of these papers) across five types:

\subsubsection{Fuzzy Overlap Scoring}

Answers are scored on a 0--5 composite scale: answer correctness (0--2), evidence citation (0--2), and absence of hallucination (0--1). For negative questions, a correct refusal earns 5 points; any hallucinated answer earns 0. Scoring uses fuzzy word-overlap matching (threshold 0.4) against manually written key points.

\subsubsection{LLM-as-Judge}

As a complementary quality metric, we deploy an LLM-as-judge using Nemotron 3 Super Free via Ollama Cloud (temperature = 0). The judge receives the question, the generated answer, the retrieved chunks, and the ground-truth answer, and assigns a score on a rigid 0--5 rubric:

\begin{itemize}
    \item 0: Completely incorrect or hallucinated
    \item 1--2: Partially correct, facts missing
    \item 3: Correct but no source citation
    \item 4: Correct with partial citation
    \item 5: Complete, precise, with explicit document citation
\end{itemize}

The LLM judge is applied to all 9 model $\times$ pipeline combinations (50 questions each). Fuzzy overlap and LLM judge are compared via Pearson and Spearman correlation.

\subsubsection{Oracle Test}

An oracle test provides an upper bound on retrieval-quality improvement: for 30 of 50 questions, we replace the top-3 retrieved chunks with the full raw text of the correct document, bypassing ChromaDB entirely. Negative questions and questions affected by API errors are excluded.

\subsubsection{Bootstrap and Permutation Tests}

We compute paired bootstrap confidence intervals (10,000 resamples, SEED = 42) on the per-question score differences ($\Delta_i = \text{score}_A(i) - \text{score}_B(i)$) and permutation tests that randomize pipeline labels within each question pair to estimate p-values under the null hypothesis of no pipeline effect.

\subsubsection{Error Taxonomy}

We complement aggregate scores with a 7-code error taxonomy (E01--E07, see Appendix~\ref{sec:taxonomy}) that classifies each suboptimal answer by origin: retrieval failure (E01: correct document not in top-3; E02: document retrieved but specific chunk missing), generation failure (E03: context ignored; E04: citation missing; E05: hallucination; E06: false refusal), and scoring artifact (E07: word-overlap mismatch).

\subsection{Shallow Chunks: The Mechanism}

\begin{figure}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.92\linewidth]{figure3_shallow}
    \caption{Distribution of retrieved chunk lengths (informative text, Markdown syntax stripped). Pipeline A (Raw) has no chunks $\leq$200 characters. Pipeline B (Markdown) has 38/150 (25.3\%) shallow chunks; inset zooms on the 0--500 range.}
    \label{fig:shallow}
\end{figure}

To investigate \emph{why} Markdown underperforms, we analyzed the content of retrieved chunks. The Markdown pipeline introduces a class of retrieval candidates we call \textbf{shallow chunks}:

\begin{quote}
\textbf{Definition.} A chunk is \emph{shallow} if, after removing Markdown syntax tokens (\texttt{\#}, \texttt{*}, \texttt{`}, \texttt{|}, \texttt{---}, \texttt{[}, \texttt{]}, \texttt{>}) and trimming whitespace, the remaining text has $\leq$200 characters.
\end{quote}

These chunks typically consist of section headers alone, short list items, or table formatting artifacts. In our base corpus benchmark, \textbf{25.3\% of retrieved Markdown chunks (38/150)} are shallow, versus \textbf{0\% for the Raw pipeline}. The embedding model matches these near-empty chunks to queries via lexical overlap in structural words: ``Transformer'', ``Model'', ``Attention'' in section headers produce high cosine similarity scores against queries containing those keywords, despite contributing no factual content.

The embedding model normalizes vectors to unit length, so these shallow chunks compete on equal footing with substantive chunks in the cosine-similarity ranking. The structural words that make Markdown informative for human readers become \textbf{embedding false positives} in retrieval, diluting the quality of the top-3 context window fed to the generator.

\subsection{Pipeline C: Removing Shallow Chunks}

To test whether shallow chunks causally harm retrieval, we implemented Pipeline C that filters out chunks with $\leq$200 informative characters before indexing. On the base corpus (50 questions), the filter removed 9 chunks (1.6\% of the 553-chunk Markdown collection). For comparison, 25.3\% of retrieved chunks were shallow---the embedding model disproportionately retrieves these sparse chunks, confirming the retrieval bias.

Table~\ref{tab:gradient} shows improvement across all three reliably evaluated models: +0.07 (DeepSeek V4), +0.09 (Nemotron 3), +0.17 (Gemma 3 4B). The improvement is directionally consistent but modest. Pipeline C remains below Pipeline A (Raw) for all three models ($-$0.09 to $-$0.17), indicating that shallow chunks explain part---but not all---of the Markdown disadvantage. Other factors (e.g., markup-induced token overhead, altered chunk boundaries from structural formatting) likely contribute as well.

\textbf{Mechanism confirmed.} The fact that removing only 9 chunks (1.6\% of the collection) produces measurable improvements confirms that shallow chunks are disproportionately harmful during retrieval.

\textbf{Sensitivity analysis.} A threshold sweep across T = 100, 150, 200, 250, 300 confirmed that T = 200 captures all shallow chunks without false positives. T = 100/150 filter only 5 chunks; T = 200/250/300 all filter the same 9 chunks, showing a plateau.

Two findings emerge: (1) the LLM judge is \textbf{systematically more severe} than fuzzy overlap across all models and pipelines ($\Delta$ from $-$0.21 to $-$1.20), and (2) the \textbf{correlation between the two metrics is weak to moderate} (Pearson $r$ = 0.25--0.48 for Gemma 3 4B and DeepSeek; near zero for Nemotron 3). Notably, the pipeline preference (Raw $>$ Markdown $>$ MD-filtered) is preserved by the LLM judge for Gemma 3 4B and DeepSeek V4, but reversed for Nemotron 3 (where the judge scores MD-filtered above Raw), highlighting that scoring method can interact with model identity.

These results indicate that fuzzy overlap and LLM judge measure \textbf{different aspects of answer quality} and should be treated as complementary rather than substitutable. Fuzzy overlap is deterministic and cheap but may miss semantically valid answers; the LLM judge is more holistic but introduces its own biases and is computationally expensive.

Third, questions about synthetic documents (Q064--Q075) received systematically lower scores because the LLM's parametric knowledge conflicts with the invented content---validating the synthetic paper approach for testing retrieval reliance.

The expansion result underscores that the shallow-chunk phenomenon is \textbf{document-dependent}: Markdown preprocessing is most harmful when source PDFs are rich in structural markers (section headers, lists, tables) that produce shallow chunks. For documents with simpler structure, the disadvantage is attenuated.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Why All Models Prefer Raw Text}

For all models in our benchmark (4B to 26B), Markdown's cost---25.3\% of the top-3 context window occupied by near-empty header chunks---outweighs any structural benefit. The embedding budget spent on Markdown syntax is a deadweight loss: structural keywords inflate cosine similarity for shallow chunks without adding factual content. Pipeline C confirms this: removing just 1.6\% of chunks measurably improves retrieval quality.

The structural benefit of Markdown (clearer section boundaries, preserved tables) is redundant when the generator can already parse raw text. Our finding is consistent across four providers, two embedding models, and two scoring methods.

\subsection{Practical Implications}

For RAG system designers, the implication is clear: \textbf{raw text extraction is simpler, faster, and at least as effective as Markdown preprocessing} across a wide range of generator capabilities and robustness conditions. A hybrid strategy---Markdown preprocessing followed by a minimum-content filter that removes chunks with $\leq$200 informative characters---partially recovers the loss on structurally rich corpora but does not consistently match raw text quality.

If Markdown structure is needed for other downstream tasks (e.g., citation anchoring, section-level retrieval), the shallow chunk filter is a low-cost safeguard on structurally complex documents, but its benefit may be negligible on documents that produce few shallow chunks (as observed in the expanded corpus).

\section{Limitations}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Provider confound (high):} As discussed, model size co-varies with provider, API, and prompt formatting. A controlled family study (e.g., Llama~3 1B vs.\ 8B vs.\ 70B via the same API) would isolate the size effect.

    \item \textbf{Single embedding dimension (moderate):} Both tested models (MiniLM, bge) are 384d models. Larger embedding models (e.g., 768d or 1024d) may interact differently with Markdown structure.

    \item \textbf{Small benchmark (moderate):} 75 questions on 20 documents provide limited statistical power. Per-type breakdowns (n = 5--23) are diagnostic, not confirmatory. Larger benchmarks are needed for robust inference.

    \item \textbf{Scoring proxy (moderate):} Fuzzy word-overlap is cheap and deterministic but coarse. The LLM judge is more holistic but introduces its own biases. The weak correlation between them (Pearson $r$ = 0.25--0.48) confirms they measure different aspects of quality.

    \item \textbf{Single Markdown pipeline:} The Markdown preprocessing tested here is one specific rule-based compiler. A different compiler (e.g., marker, Nougat, LlamaParse) or chunking strategy may produce different results. We do not claim that Markdown preprocessing \emph{in general} harms RAG---only that this particular instantiation does under these conditions.

    \item \textbf{Synthetic document limitations:} The synthetic documents are short (1--4 pages) and may not exhibit the same shallow-chunk profile as real PDFs. Their primary value is in testing retrieval reliance through invented facts, not in simulating realistic preprocessing conditions.

    \item \textbf{Single model for expanded corpus:} Only Gemma 3 4B was tested on the 75-question expanded corpus due to API constraints. Other models may show different patterns.

    \item \textbf{Single evaluation language:} All questions are in Italian to reduce parametric knowledge contamination. Results may differ for English-language benchmarks.

    \item \textbf{Famous papers bias (diminished):} The base corpus consists of well-known arXiv papers. The expanded corpus partly addresses this with obscure papers and synthetic documents, but residual parametric knowledge effects cannot be ruled out.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion}

We present a 4-model, 3-pipeline benchmark assessing whether compiling PDFs into Markdown before indexing improves RAG quality. Within our experimental conditions, the answer is consistently negative across all robustness checks.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{All four models prefer raw text over Markdown} ($-$3.6\% to $-$6.0\%), with partial recovery when shallow structural chunks are filtered. The Markdown penalty is robust across providers, embedding models, and scoring methods.

    \item The mechanism is \textbf{shallow chunks}: 25.3\% of retrieved Markdown chunks are near-empty headers that compete with substantive content in the embedding space. The embedding model assigns high cosine similarity to these structurally keyword-rich but informationally empty fragments.

    \item A \textbf{causal intervention} (Pipeline C) validates this mechanism: filtering shallow chunks before indexing partially recovers the loss (+0.07 to +0.17 across three models), though filtered Markdown remains below raw text quality.

    \item \textbf{Robustness checks} across embedding model ($\Delta$ = 0.12/5.0) and scoring method (LLM judge) confirm the stability of the findings.

    \item \textbf{Corpus expansion} (10 $\rightarrow$ 20 documents, 50 $\rightarrow$ 75 questions) shows the Raw advantage narrows but persists directionally ($\Delta$ = +0.13, p = 0.389). The shallow-chunk effect is document-dependent: it is strongest on structurally rich PDFs and attenuated on simpler documents.

    \item The \textbf{oracle test} confirms that retrieval quality (not generation quality) is the dominant constraint, with a +67--85\% ceiling improvement when retrieval is bypassed.
\end{itemize}

The central finding is the identification and causal confirmation of the shallow chunk retrieval failure mode---a specific, measurable mechanism by which document preprocessing degrades RAG performance. For the practitioner, the recommendation is clear: raw text extraction is simpler, faster, and at least as effective as Markdown preprocessing for typical RAG systems. If Markdown structure is required for other purposes, a minimum-content filter is a low-cost safeguard on structurally complex documents, though its benefit may be negligible on documents with low structural density.
